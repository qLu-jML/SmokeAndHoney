# Setting & World Overview


Smoke & Honey is set in rural Millhaven County, Iowa — rolling hills, river bottomland, timber edges, and the vast patchwork of corn and soybean fields that define the Midwest agricultural landscape. The world is not open-world free-roam. It is a set of interconnected handcrafted scene maps that the player navigates between, each with its own forage character, NPC connections, and expansion potential.

The world grows with the player. In Year 1, the player sees only their home property and the road to town. By Year 4, they have relationships and apiaries across multiple distinct environments, each unlocked through NPC quests and earned reputation.


| Region | Rural Millhaven County, Iowa — a fictional Midwest state inspired by the river valleys, hardwood timber, and farm country of the Midwest, hardwood timber, and agricultural landscape. Not a real place, but every detail feels like it could be. |
| --- | --- |
| Tone | Warm, quiet, real. No fantasy elements. The drama is in the seasons and the bees. |
| Navigation | Scene-based. Player travels between locations via a simple map screen. Travel costs time and a small amount of energy. |
| Time of Day | Each scene reflects the current in-game time and season. Morning light in spring feels different from a gray fall afternoon. |
| Scale | Locations are intimate — you see your hives, your yard, your road. Not satellite-view. Think over-the-shoulder farm sim scale. |


### The Player Character — Eli

Twelve years old. Eldest of three — a younger brother (8) and a younger sister (5). Good family. Mom's a dental hygienist, Dad drives for a regional freight company. Lower middle class, small suburban house, decent school. Nothing is broken. Nobody is cruel.

But Eli is the oldest, and the oldest always hears more than they're supposed to.

He hears the news his parents leave on in the kitchen. He reads the things his classmates share in group chats — headlines about AI replacing jobs, wars escalating overseas, climate reports that read like countdown clocks. He's twelve, so he doesn't have the context to sort signal from noise. He just has all of it, all the time, vibrating at a frequency that grown-ups have learned to tune out but kids haven't.

The specific fear isn't "robots will kill us" — it's something quieter and worse. It's the feeling that the future is **unsettled**. That the adults are making it up as they go. That nobody actually knows if things are going to be okay, and the people who say they do are just louder, not more right.

Eli doesn't have the vocabulary for "existential dread." He just knows that doing his homework feels pointless when an AI can do it better, that saving his allowance feels pointless when money might not work the same way in ten years, and that making plans feels like drawing on water.

He hasn't told anyone this. His parents would say "you're too young to worry about that" — which he knows is a non-answer, because the world doesn't check your age before it changes. His younger siblings are still in the uncomplicated part. His friends are either oblivious or performing calmness they don't feel.

So Eli does what a lot of smart, anxious twelve-year-olds do: he retreats into screens. Not because he loves them, but because they're **numbing**. Scrolling is easier than sitting with the feeling. Games are easier than thinking. The blue light is a painkiller for a kind of pain nobody taught him to name.

His parents notice. They're not sure what to do. He's not in trouble. His grades are fine. He's just... dimmer than he used to be. Quieter. Present in the room but absent from the conversation. They talk about it at night when the kids are asleep, and they don't have answers either.

The player has no prescribed visual appearance beyond what the sprite suggests. He is defined entirely by his decisions in the world. The game never tells him what kind of beekeeper to be — only what the bees need.

### Uncle Bob — Robert "Bob" Whitfield

Late 50s. Dad's older brother. A man who has lived alone since his wife passed eight years ago and filled the silence with bees. He's not sad — he's settled. He chose this life deliberately. A small property, a workshop, a dozen hives, a truck that starts on the third try, and a conviction that the world makes more sense at the pace of seasons than at the pace of headlines.

He had a cardiac event in December — a minor heart attack that scared him more than he'll admit. He spent three weeks recovering while the temperature dropped to negative fifteen. When he finally got out to the apiary, eleven of his twelve hives were dead. Frozen. Silent boxes in the snow. One hive survived — the strongest colony, the one with the queen he'd been most proud of.

Bob is not a man who asks for help. Calling his brother's family was the hardest phone call he's made since the funeral. He didn't ask for a farmhand. He asked for Eli specifically, because he sees something in the kid — the same restless intelligence that Bob had at twelve, before he found the bees and the bees found him.

Uncle Bob is not a quest-giver. He is a **philosophy delivered through action**. He never lectures Eli about screens or anxiety or the news cycle. He just hands him a hive tool and says "let's see what the girls are up to."

Everything Bob teaches about bees is also, quietly, about living:


| What Bob says about bees | What Eli hears about life |
| --- | --- |
| "You can't rush an inspection. The bees know if you're anxious." | Slow down. Be present. Anxiety is contagious. |
| "A good beekeeper doesn't control the hive. He reads it." | You can't control the future. You can learn to read what's in front of you. |
| "Winter kills hives. That's not failure. That's winter." | Loss is part of the deal. Surviving it isn't about prevention — it's about preparation. |
| "The queen doesn't know she's important. She just does her work." | Purpose isn't something you find. It's something you do. |
| "Eleven hives died. One made it. We start from the one." | You don't need the whole world to be okay. You need one thing that's alive and worth tending. |


Bob's heart condition doesn't disappear. It's present throughout the game as a **quiet background pressure** — not a ticking clock, not a tragedy engine, but a persistent reminder that people you love are mortal and time with them is not guaranteed. Bob gets tired more easily. He sits on the tailgate while Eli does the heavy lifting. Some weeks he's great — cracking jokes, working alongside the kid, showing him queen cells with boyish excitement. Other weeks he's slower, quieter, taking his pills with his morning coffee and waving off questions. The player never gets a "Bob's health meter." They just notice. The same way Eli notices.

### The Call

It happens on an ordinary Tuesday in late winter. Eli is on the couch, earbuds in, watching something he won't remember. His mom answers the phone in the kitchen. He hears her voice change — not panicked, but the specific kind of careful-gentle that adults use when someone they love is hurting.

She sits down across from Eli and takes one earbud out.

*"That was Uncle Bob."*

Eli knows Uncle Bob. Dad's older brother. Lives alone on a small property out in Millhaven County, Iowa. Keeps bees. Always smells like wood smoke and sends jars of honey at Christmas that are better than anything in a store. He's a quiet man who knows how to fix things and doesn't say much he doesn't mean.

*"He had some heart trouble this winter. He's okay — he's okay — but he can't do the heavy work right now. And... the winter got most of his hives. He's down to one."*

Eli doesn't know much about bees. But he knows what Uncle Bob's voice sounds like when he talks about them — the only time the quiet man isn't quiet. And he knows that "down to one" means something was lost that mattered.

*"He asked if maybe you'd want to come out for the summer. Help him get back on his feet. He said he could teach you."*

And here is the moment. Not dramatic. Not cinematic. Just a twelve-year-old on a couch in a suburban house, earbuds dangling, looking at his mom, and feeling — for the first time in months — something that isn't dread.

*"Yeah. I want to go."*

He says it fast. Before he can think about it. Before the part of his brain that's been running worst-case scenarios for six months can weigh in. He says yes because Uncle Bob needs him, and needing is concrete, and concrete is the thing he's been starving for.

**Design Note — Why Eli says yes so fast:** This is the narrative crux and it needs to land emotionally in the first five minutes of gameplay. Eli doesn't say yes because he loves bees. He doesn't say yes because he's brave. He says yes because **someone asked him for something real.** The AI can't help Uncle Bob. The wars don't matter in Millhaven County. The algorithms don't know how to read a brood frame. For the first time in months, Eli is looking at a problem that is physical, present, and solvable by a person who shows up and pays attention.

### The Surviving Hive — One Box in the Snow

When Eli arrives at Uncle Bob's property for the first time, he sees the apiary: twelve hive stands in two rows of six, arranged in a gentle arc near the tree line. Eleven are empty. The lids are off. The boxes are hollow and still. One hive — the one at the far left, closest to the morning sun — has a small cluster of bees moving at the entrance.

That's the whole game. That's the starting condition. **One living hive in a graveyard of boxes.**

The surviving hive is not a gameplay convenience. It is the **central metaphor of the entire game**. Eleven things died. One thing lived. And the question is not "why did this happen" — the question is "what do you do with the one that made it?"

Eli's existential crisis is the same shape: the world feels like it's full of dead hives. Things that were working stopped working. Certainties that were alive went cold. But one thing is still here, still humming, still alive. And it needs him.

The game's opening tutorial is built around the first time Eli opens this hive with Uncle Bob standing behind him. It's early spring — Quickening. The colony is small, maybe 8,000 bees (down from a healthy 40,000+). The queen is laying, but slowly. The stores are thin. The brood pattern is patchy but present. Uncle Bob doesn't sugarcoat it: *"She's alive. That's more than I had any right to expect. But she's weak. She needs room, she needs food, and she needs somebody checking on her every week. Think you can do that?"*

### The Eleven Dead Hives — Loss as Setting

When Eli arrives, the eleven dead hives are physically present. Empty Langstroth boxes with the lids removed, stacked on their stands, slowly weathering. They are the first thing the player sees in the apiary and they never fully go away — even as the player fills them with new colonies, the memory of what was lost is baked into the landscape.

They serve three functions simultaneously:

**Narrative function:** They are the visible evidence of what happened while Bob was recovering. Each empty box is a colony that was alive in October and dead by January. The player doesn't see this loss happen — they see the aftermath. It's more powerful that way.

**Gameplay function:** The empty equipment is usable. The player doesn't have to buy eleven hive bodies — they're already there, waiting to be cleaned, repaired, and filled with new bees. The dead hives become the infrastructure for the rebuild. What killed becomes what carries forward.

**Thematic function:** Eleven dead, one alive. The world Eli came from is full of things that feel dead or dying — certainties, institutions, plans. Uncle Bob's apiary is the same, in miniature. And the game's answer is the same too: you don't mourn the eleven forever. You start with the one. You rebuild from what survived.

### The Narrative Arc — By Year

Smoke & Honey is not a story about bees. It's a story about **what happens when a person who feels powerless discovers that attention is a form of power.**


| Year | Eli's internal state | What the bees teach him |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Year 1 | Anxious, uncertain, present only because Uncle Bob needs him. Still checking his phone in the evenings. Still carrying the weight of a world that feels like it's ending. | Living things need you to show up whether you feel like it or not. The hive doesn't care about your mood. It needs food, space, and attention. And the act of giving those things makes you feel less empty, not more. |
| Year 2 | Growing confidence. Starting to notice things before Bob tells him. The phone stays in his pocket longer. He has calluses. He sleeps better. | Knowledge is earned, not downloaded. An AI could list the symptoms of American Foulbrood, but Eli learned to smell it. The difference between information and understanding is your hands in the hive. |
| Year 3 | Ownership. This isn't Bob's apiary anymore — it's theirs. Eli makes decisions without asking. He disagrees with Bob sometimes and is right. He's teaching his younger brother to hold a smoker on a summer visit. | You can build something real in a world that feels unreal. The honey in the jars came from flowers Eli planted, in hives Eli built, from bees Eli kept alive through a winter everyone said would be too hard. |
| Year 4 | The dread is still there — the world didn't get less scary. But Eli holds it differently now. It's not the whole sky. It's weather. And he's learned that you don't control weather. You prepare for it, you endure it, and you check the hives in the morning. | Mastery doesn't mean the fear goes away. It means you have something to do with your hands while you carry it. |
| Year 5 | Eli is seventeen. He's breeding queens. The apiary has fifteen hives. Uncle Bob sits on the porch more than he works the yard. When Eli's little sister asks if he's scared about the future, he says: "Yeah. But I've got work to do in the morning." | One surviving hive became fifteen. One scared kid became a beekeeper. The world is still loud. The bees are still quiet. And quiet things, tended well, outlast the noise. |


### Narrative Design Rules

Smoke & Honey is a simulation first and a narrative second. The story is **never told through cutscenes that interrupt gameplay**. It is told through the gameplay itself, and through small, earned moments between tasks.


| Rule | Rationale |
| --- | --- |
| No opening cinematic longer than 90 seconds | The phone call, the arrival, the first look at the apiary. That's all the player needs before they're holding a hive tool. The backstory fills in through dialogue and observation over weeks, not a front-loaded info dump. |
| Bob's dialogue is earned, not triggered | Bob talks when Eli does something worth talking about — a good inspection, a bad decision, a first harvest. He doesn't monologue. His best lines happen while he's doing something else. |
| Eli's internal state is shown, never told | No narration boxes saying "Eli felt less anxious." Instead: Eli stops checking his phone during inspections. His journal entries get longer and more observant. His walking animation gradually shifts from hunched to upright. The player feels the change because they made it happen. |
| The existential theme is subtext, not text | The game never says "the bees cured my anxiety about AI." Eli's fear of the future is established in the opening, referenced occasionally in dialogue, and quietly resolved through the act of playing. The player draws the connection themselves. |
| The surviving hive is the player's hive | The metaphor only works if the player has emotional ownership of the hive. If they lose it, that has to hurt. If they grow it to ten colonies, that has to feel like a personal victory, not a progression bar filling up. |
| Uncle Bob can have bad weeks | Some weeks, Bob is slower. He sits instead of standing. He forgets something he told the player last week. These moments are uncommented — no health bar, no quest notification. The player just notices, the way you notice with people you love. |
| The phone as narrative device | Eli has a phone. Early game, he checks it during inspections (a subtle idle animation). As the game progresses, this animation becomes less frequent and eventually stops. If the player opens the phone in the pause menu, the headlines are real — AI breakthroughs, wars, economic uncertainty. The phone is not the enemy. It's a window into the noise that Eli is learning to live alongside, not escape from. |


**Design Note:** The game's message is not "technology bad, nature good." It's: you need something that roots you, so the noise becomes weather instead of the whole sky. The backstory framing does several things at once: it explains the starting hive (one survivor from a devastating winter), the equipment (eleven empty boxes ready to rebuild), the mentor connection (Uncle Bob needs help), and the emotional stakes (a kid finding purpose). It gives the location emotional weight — this is not just a business. The player is caretaking something alive, for someone they love, in a world that feels like it's falling apart. The drama is in whether they rise to it.

#### World Tone and Setting Rules


| Rule | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| No fantasy elements | No magic, no anthropomorphized bees with personalities, no supernatural events. The drama is entirely in the natural world. |
| Present-day, unspecified year | Technology matches roughly 2020s rural Midwest. Smartphones exist but are not shown. No specific year is ever stated. |
| The Cedar Bend calendar is in-world fiction | Month names (Quickening, Greening, etc.) are in-game names used by locals. They map precisely to real seasons. They exist for charm and immersion, not to obscure real-world parallels. |
| Climate is temperate continental | Inspired by the river valleys of the upper Midwest and Mid-Atlantic: four hard seasons, periodic spring flooding, hot humid summers, cold winters with occasional mild spells. Not the Deep South, not New England. |
| Scale is intimate | The world is small and knowable. You see the same people repeatedly. The town knows who you are by Year 2. This is intentional — it is what makes every interaction meaningful. |
