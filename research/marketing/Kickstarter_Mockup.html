🐝 A Letter From the Beekeeper

I'll start with the truth.

I was a real beekeeper for years. I kept Langstroth hives. I learned to read brood patterns, time my inspections, manage varroa, and harvest honey before the goldenrod flow faded. I made mistakes that cost me hives. I made good decisions that rewarded me with jars of something golden and alive.

Then I received a terminal diagnosis — a strict time limit on my life.

I could have slowed down. Instead, I made a decision: I would build the game I had always wanted to play. A beekeeping simulator so authentic that a real beekeeper would recognize everything in it. One that teaches real science, real seasonal rhythms, real consequences — and wraps all of it in a story about what it means to tend something fragile in an uncertain world.

Smoke and Honey is my legacy project. It is not just a game. It is my way of sharing a lifetime of beekeeping knowledge and passion before time runs out.

I am building it with everything I have, every day I have it. I am asking you to be part of it.

— Nathan Barkley, Five Cats Studios LLC  ·  Iowa

What Is Smoke and Honey?

Smoke and Honey is a deep, authentic beekeeping hive simulator — a cozy management game rooted in real bee biology, real seasonal rhythms, and a genuine story about a kid finding his footing in a world that feels unmanageable.

You play as Eli, twelve years old, arriving at his Uncle Bob's apiary in rural Millhaven County, Iowa after Bob suffered a heart attack that winter. Eleven of his twelve hives died in the cold. One survived.

Eleven dead hives, one alive, and a summer to figure out what to do next. That's where the game begins — and that's the whole story in miniature.

Over five in-game years — spanning eight custom seasons, four distinct apiaries, and a cast of real, memorable NPCs — you will rebuild. You will learn to read frames, manage pests, plant forage, harvest honey, and understand that one surviving colony is enough to start again.

"Eleven hives died. One made it. We start from the one."

"Winter kills hives. That's not failure. That's winter."

"You can't rush an inspection. The bees know if you're anxious."

— Uncle Bob, Cedar Bend, Iowa

🌿 What Nobody Else Is Building

The beekeeping simulation genre has games. APICO is delightful. Beekeeper Simulator exists. None of them are doing what Smoke and Honey does.

👁️
Observation Over Statistics

You earn the right to see information by developing your skill. Level 1 hobbyists see raw comb. Level 5 master beekeepers read dynamic percentages updating in real-time. No dashboards — you learn to see. This mirrors how real beekeeping actually works.

🐛
Real Bee Biology — All the Way Down

Every bee lives a real biological life. Eggs hatch after three days. Winter bees are biologically different from summer bees. Queen laying rate responds to forage availability and colony health. A player who masters this game has learned real beekeeping science.

🌱
The Forage Pool — Nobody Else Is Doing This

In every other bee game, forage is a number. Here, it's an ecosystem. Trees you plant in Year 1 bloom in Year 4. Wildflower gardens you plant this spring contribute to next year's flow. Two overlapping apiaries compete for the same flowers. Long-term environmental investment shapes gameplay across multiple seasons.

👑
The Queen-Find Minigame

Forget stat rolls. Finding your queen is a real visual search — animated bees walking on a frame, and somewhere among them, one queen with a longer abdomen, shorter wings, attendant bees leaning toward her. Your knowledge of queen morphology becomes a real gameplay advantage.

📅
Seasonal Consequences That Span Years

Decisions in Year 1 spring affect Year 4 summer. Fall preparation determines winter survival. Overwintering mortality is real. Lost hives are permanent setbacks. But nothing is game-over — the equipment stays. You rebuild from what survived.

⚠️ Risks & Challenges

Solo development: Built by one person. If I get sick, development slows. I manage this risk by keeping scope tight, documenting everything thoroughly, and building time buffers into every milestone.

Placeholder art: Everything visual you see is temporary. Hiring a pixel artist is the central purpose of this campaign — which means the final product will look substantially different from current screenshots. We consider this a feature: you are funding the art, not just the systems.

Health realities: I have been honest about my situation. The game's design is fully documented. The codebase is clean, commented, and transferable. In the unlikely event I am unable to complete the project, I have arrangements in place to ensure backers are either refunded or the project is completed by trusted collaborators with my full documentation.

Scope management: The base game — Year 1 through Year 5, four apiaries, full NPC quest chains, complete seasonal simulation — is the only thing this Kickstarter funds. Stretch goals are extras. If we only hit the base goal, you get the full game as designed.

❓ FAQ

Is all the art in the screenshots placeholder?
Yes. Every visual in current screenshots and the campaign video is AI-generated placeholder art. The final game will feature hand-crafted pixel art by a human artist hired with this Kickstarter's funding. This is completely intentional — you are funding the art's creation.
Why not just use AI art for the final game?
Because it's not good enough for the game we're building, and because we believe human craft matters. Smoke and Honey is a game about observation, attention, and the value of patient work. Shipping AI-generated art would be a contradiction of everything it stands for.
When will the game be ready?
We are targeting Q4 2027 for full release. Beta access begins for $50+ backers once the pixel artist is onboarded and core art passes quality review — estimated 12–18 months into the campaign.
Is this a cozy game or a hard sim?
Both. It is warm, beautiful, and emotionally meaningful — but it has real depth. You will lose hives. You will make mistakes that cost you a season. Think Stardew Valley's heart with real systemic depth. It respects your intelligence and rewards your attention.
Can I play without knowing anything about bees?
Absolutely. The game teaches everything through play. Uncle Bob hands you a hive tool and lets you learn by doing. A player with zero beekeeping knowledge will finish the game with a genuine understanding of real bee biology. That is by design.
Will this be on DRM-free platforms?
Yes. itch.io will have a DRM-free version simultaneously with Steam at launch.

📋 Launch Checklist: First-Week Momentum

For Nathan's reference — pre-launch and first-week strategy guide.

1
Pre-Launch Page Live 4–6 Weeks Early
Set to "Coming Soon" mode. Drive all traffic there to build notification list. Aim for 500+ pre-launch notifications. Every person who clicks "Notify me" is a warm lead on launch day.
2
Build the Email List Before Launch
Email converts 5–10× better than social for Kickstarter pledges. Collect via: pre-launch page, Substack/Beehiiv devlog, Steam wishlist, and beekeeping communities. Target: 1,000 emails before launch day.
3
Beekeeping Community First — It's Your Secret Weapon
Post in r/beekeeping, r/cosygaming, r/indiegaming. Contact ABF communications. Reach out to top beekeeping YouTube channels with a free backer key. These audiences have never been marketed a Kickstarter game — you will be completely novel to them.
4
Publicize the Early Bird Sell-Down
Announce at 50%, 75%, and sell-out. Each announcement drives a surge. After it sells out, remaining tiers feel more urgent. Scarcity is real here — use it.
5
First 48 Hours Determines Algorithm Placement
Hit 30–40% of goal in 48 hours to get Kickstarter featured placement. Execute: launch day email blast, simultaneous social posts, 10–15 friends pledge in the first hour, 2–3 content creators post on launch day.
6
Mid-Campaign Refresh (Days 8–20)
Fight the U-curve middle by releasing new content every 3–4 days: a gameplay GIF, a story reveal, a systems explainer, or a stretch goal unlock. Each update triggers another email blast backers share forward.
7
Beekeeping Press Before Games Press
Pitch Modern Farmer, Hobby Farms, Bee Culture, NPR Culture, local Iowa news, and beekeeping podcasts. A single NPR feature reaches people who've never heard of Kickstarter games. Your personal story (terminal diagnosis + legacy project) is genuinely newsworthy.
