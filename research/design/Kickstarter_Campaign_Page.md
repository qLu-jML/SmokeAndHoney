## 🐝 A Letter From the Beekeeper

I'll start with the truth.

I was a real beekeeper for years. I kept Langstroth hives. I learned to read brood patterns, time my inspections, manage varroa, and harvest honey before the goldenrod flow faded. I made mistakes that cost me hives. I made good decisions that rewarded me with jars of something golden and alive.

Then I received a terminal diagnosis — a strict time limit on my life.

I could have slowed down. Instead, I made a decision: I would build the game I had always wanted to play. A beekeeping simulator so authentic that a real beekeeper would recognize everything in it. One that teaches real science, real seasonal rhythms, real consequences — and wraps all of it in a story about what it means to tend something fragile in an uncertain world.

**Smoke and Honey is my legacy project.** It is not just a game. It is my way of sharing a lifetime of beekeeping knowledge and passion before time runs out.

I am building it with everything I have, every day I have it.

I am asking you to be part of it.

## What Is Smoke and Honey?

**Smoke and Honey** is a deep, authentic beekeeping hive simulator — a cozy management game rooted in real bee biology, real seasonal rhythms, and a genuine story about a kid finding his footing in a world that feels unmanageable.

You play as **Eli**, twelve years old, arriving at his Uncle Bob's apiary in rural Millhaven County, Iowa after Bob suffered a heart attack that winter. Eleven of his twelve hives died in the cold. One survived.

That's where the game begins: **eleven dead hives, one alive, and a summer to figure out what to do next.**

Over five in-game years — spanning eight custom seasons, four distinct apiaries, and a cast of real, memorable NPCs — you will rebuild. You will learn to read frames, manage pests, plant forage, harvest honey, and understand why a dying bee colony isn't the end of the story.

**This is not a game where you click buttons and numbers go up.**

This is a game where you learn to *observe*.

---

## 🌿 The World Nobody Else Is Building

The beekeeping simulation genre has games. APICO is delightful. Beekeeper Simulator exists. But none of them are doing what Smoke and Honey does. Here's what makes us different:

---

### Observation Over Statistics

Most management games hand you dashboards, charts, and tooltips. Smoke and Honey does something different: **you earn the right to see information by developing your skill.**

- A Level 1 hobbyist sees raw comb — no labels, no numbers. Just what's there.
- A Level 3 beekeeper gets qualitative descriptions: "solid brood pattern," "laying queen present."
- A Level 5 master sees dynamic percentages that update in real-time as you examine each frame side.

This mirrors how real beekeeping works. You don't get a readout. You learn to see.

---

### Real Bee Biology — All the Way Down

Every bee in every hive lives a real life. Eggs hatch after three days. Larvae are capped at nine. New workers emerge after twenty-one days as house bees, transition to nurses, and finally become foragers — who wear out their wings and die in the field.

- Queen laying rate responds dynamically to forage availability, available space, and colony health
- Varroa mite populations grow inside capped brood — untreated colonies spiral
- Winter bees are biologically different from summer bees; they must exist in sufficient numbers before fall or the colony dies
- Population structure determines hive function — a 40,000-bee colony with the wrong age distribution fails just like a weak one

**A player who masters Smoke and Honey has learned real beekeeping science.**

---

### The Forage Pool — Nobody Else Is Doing This

In every other bee game, forage is a number. In Smoke and Honey, **forage is an ecosystem.**

Each apiary has a dynamic forage pool — a real-time simulation of available nectar and pollen within a 1–2 mile radius. Blooming plants fill that pool seasonally. The pool depletes as your foragers work it. Two overlapping apiaries compete for the same flowers.

**And here's what makes it special:** trees you plant in Year 1 bloom in Year 4. Wildflower gardens you establish in the first spring provide consistent flow in Year 2 and beyond. A quest with a neighboring farmer can shift an entire crop rotation — changing your forage character for years.

*This creates the only beekeeping game where long-term environmental investment shapes gameplay across multiple seasons.*

---

### Eleven Dead Hives — One Alive

The eleven empty Langstroth boxes at Uncle Bob's apiary never disappear. They weather slowly. They become equipment. They become reminder.

**They are simultaneously:**
- Free gear for your rebuild (the boxes are yours; clean them, fill them)
- Visible proof of what happened that winter
- A quiet metaphor at the center of the whole game

Eli doesn't mourn the eleven forever. He starts with the one. The game doesn't end when things go wrong — it changes.

---

### A Story That Earns Its Emotion

Uncle Bob doesn't lecture. He hands Eli a hive tool and says: *"Let's see what the girls are up to."*

Everything Bob teaches about bees is quietly about living:

- *"You can't rush an inspection. The bees know if you're anxious."* — Slow down. Be present.
- *"A good beekeeper doesn't control the hive. He reads it."* — You can't control the future.
- *"Winter kills hives. That's not failure. That's winter."* — Loss is part of the deal.
- *"Eleven hives died. One made it. We start from the one."* — You don't need everything to be okay. You need one thing alive and worth tending.

Bob's health is always quietly in the background — not a timer, not a tragedy machine, just a reminder that people you love are mortal and time with them is not guaranteed. Over five years, Eli changes: from a kid numbing himself with screens to a teenager who says, when asked if he's scared about the future: *"Yeah. But I've got work to do in the morning."*

---

### Real Seasonal Tension — Eight Named Months

The game runs on a custom agricultural calendar that reflects what each season actually *feels* like to a beekeeper:

| Season | Month | Feeling |
|---|---|---|
| **Quickening** | Early Spring | Anxious. Did they make it? |
| **Greening** | Late Spring | Fragile beauty. Dandelions roll. |
| **Wide-Clover** | Early Summer | Demanding abundance. Full flow. |
| **High-Sun** | Late Summer | Mid-summer dearth. Harvest window opens. |
| **Full-Earth** | Early Fall | Fulfillment. Hives are heavy. |
| **Reaping** | Late Fall | Honest reckoning. Did you prepare enough? |
| **Deepcold** | Winter | Quiet. Crafting. Waiting. |
| **Kindlemonth** | Late Winter | Light returning. Planning. Kindling. |

No two seasons feel the same. Each one asks something different of you.

---

### The Queen-Find Minigame

Forget stat rolls. Finding your queen in Smoke and Honey is a **real visual search challenge** — animated bees walking on a frame, and somewhere among them, one queen with a longer abdomen, shorter wings, and attendant bees leaning toward her.

Difficulty scales with your hive: easy colonies with Italian bees are lighter and slower; hard Russian colonies are darker and dense. Your knowledge of queen morphology — earned through gameplay — becomes a real advantage. No RNG. Just observation.

---

### No Extractive Design. No Gacha. No Premium Currency.

Every piece of progression in Smoke and Honey is earned by learning, not by spending. There are no loot boxes, no energy meters to circumvent, no rare-item gacha. Cheaper queen suppliers are viable — you just get what you pay for, which is authentic: cheaper suppliers exist because they don't cull as aggressively.

The economy is player-paced. You sell honey at the Saturday Market, build relationships with regular customers, add varietal labeling over time, learn to brew mead in Year 4. Nothing is a grind. Everything rewards attention.

# FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

**Q: Is all the art in the screenshots placeholder?**
Yes. Every visual in current screenshots and the campaign video (except the concept mood boards) is AI-generated placeholder art used to build and test the game systems. The final game will feature hand-crafted pixel art by a human artist hired with this Kickstarter's funding. We are completely transparent about this — it is the whole point.

**Q: Why not just use AI art for the final game?**
Because it's not good enough for the game we're building, and because we believe human craft matters. Smoke and Honey is a game about observation, attention, and the value of patient work. Shipping AI-generated art would be a contradiction of everything it stands for. The human pixel artist will bring something no AI can: decisions made with intentionality, a consistent vision, and personal investment in this world.

**Q: When will the game be ready?**
We are targeting Q4 2027 for full release. Beta access begins for $50+ backers once the pixel artist is onboarded and core art passes quality review (estimated 12–18 months into the campaign).

**Q: Is this a cozy game or a hard sim?**
Both. It is warm, beautiful, and emotionally meaningful — but it has real depth. You will lose hives. You will make mistakes that cost you a season. No loss is permanent (the equipment stays), but consequences are real. Think Stardew Valley's heart, Dwarf Fortress's depth, without either game's frustration. It respects your intelligence and rewards your attention.

**Q: Can I play without knowing anything about bees?**
Absolutely. The game teaches everything through play. Uncle Bob is not a wiki dump — he hands you a hive tool and lets you learn by doing. A player with zero beekeeping knowledge will finish the game with a genuine understanding of real bee biology. That is by design.

**Q: Is this game appropriate for kids?**
Yes. The game is designed for ages 12 and up, but adults will find it equally deep and emotionally resonant. The story specifically features a 12-year-old protagonist because the core themes — anxiety, uncertainty, finding purpose through craft — are universal but particularly acute for young people right now.

**Q: What if development doesn't go as planned?**
I have been transparent about my health situation. The game's design is fully documented. The code is clean and transferable. I have made arrangements with trusted collaborators to ensure that if I am unable to complete the project, backers will be either refunded or the project will be completed using my complete documentation. I am not asking you to take a leap of faith based on promises — I am asking you to look at what has already been built and make a considered decision.

**Q: Will this be on DRM-free platforms?**
Yes. itch.io will have a DRM-free version available simultaneously with Steam at launch.

**Q: What happened to the bees?**
In real life? Mine are doing fine. 🐝

## 🚀 LAUNCH CHECKLIST: Maximum First-Week Funding

### 1. Pre-Launch Page (Live 4–6 Weeks Early)
Set your Kickstarter page to "Coming Soon" mode at least 4 weeks before launch. Drive all traffic there to build the notification list before day one. Every person who clicks "Notify me at launch" is a warm lead. Aim for 500+ pre-launch notifications. Launch day emails to everyone on that list.

### 2. Email List Is Your Most Important Asset
Build it before you launch, not after. Email converts 5–10x better than social media for Kickstarter pledges. Collect emails via: the pre-launch Kickstarter page, a free devlog newsletter (Substack or Beehiiv), your Steam wishlist page (live now — link everything there), and any beekeeping or cozy game communities you've joined. Aim for 1,000 emails before launch day.

### 3. Beekeeping Community Is Your Secret Weapon
Before any other marketing: post in r/beekeeping, r/cosygaming, r/indiegaming. Join the American Beekeeping Federation's email list and reach out to their communications team. Contact the top 5–10 beekeeping YouTube channels (Beekeeping 101, The Bush Bee Man, etc.) with a free backer key and a genuine pitch. These audiences don't get indie games marketed to them. You will stand out completely.

### 4. Early Bird Tier Sells Out Quickly — Publicize It
The 200-slot Early Bird tier creates urgency and social proof. Announce when it's 50% claimed, 75% claimed, and when it sells out. Each announcement drives a mini-surge. After it sells out, backers on the fence will feel the urgency of the remaining tiers.

### 5. First 48 Hours Determines Algorithm Placement
Kickstarter's algorithm surfaces projects that fund quickly. If you hit 30–40% of goal in the first 48 hours, you get featured placement, which triggers organic discovery. To make this happen: launch day email blast to your full list, post everywhere simultaneously, have 10–15 friends and family pledge within the first hour, and have 2–3 streamers or content creators ready to post on launch day.

### 6. Mid-Campaign Refresh (Days 8–20)
Every Kickstarter follows a U-shaped curve: strong start, quiet middle, strong finish. Fight the middle by releasing a new piece of content every 3–4 days during the lull: a new gameplay GIF, a story reveal, a "behind the systems" explainer, or a stretch goal unlock. Each update triggers another email to backers who share it forward.

### 7. Press Outreach — Beekeeping Beat First, Games Press Second
Most indie games go straight to games press. Yours has a secret: the beekeeping angle. Pitch to: Modern Farmer, Hobby Farms, Bee Culture magazine, NPR's culture desk, local Iowa news, and beekeeping podcasts. A single NPR feature or Bee Culture article reaches an audience that has never heard of Kickstarter games — and they are *exactly* the people who will back this. The personal story (terminal diagnosis + legacy project + authentic beekeeping) is genuinely newsworthy. Use it.
